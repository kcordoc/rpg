# Prevention and Risk Assessment

## Understanding Your Risk

Heart disease is the #1 killer worldwide, causing about 17.9 million deaths per year. But up to 80% of premature heart disease is preventable through lifestyle changes and proper medical treatment.

### The Major Risk Factors
1. **High LDL cholesterol** — the primary driver of atherosclerosis
2. **High blood pressure (hypertension)** — damages artery walls, accelerating plaque formation
3. **Smoking** — damages blood vessels and lowers HDL
4. **Diabetes** — high blood sugar damages blood vessels and promotes inflammation
5. **Obesity** — especially visceral fat (belly fat), which is metabolically active
6. **Family history** — if a first-degree relative had heart disease before age 55 (men) or 65 (women)
7. **Age** — risk increases with age (men 45+, women 55+)
8. **Physical inactivity** — sedentary lifestyle doubles heart disease risk
9. **Chronic stress and poor sleep** — contribute to inflammation and poor metabolic health
10. **High Lp(a)** — the genetic risk factor that most people don't know about

### The 10-Year Risk Calculator
The ACC/AHA Pooled Cohort Equation estimates your 10-year risk of a heart attack or stroke:
- **Low risk:** Less than 5%
- **Borderline risk:** 5-7.5%
- **Intermediate risk:** 7.5-20%
- **High risk:** Above 20%

This calculator helps guide treatment decisions. But it has limitations — it doesn't include Lp(a), family history details, or coronary artery calcium scores.

## Coronary Artery Calcium (CAC) Score

A CAC scan is a non-contrast CT scan of the heart that measures calcified plaque in your coronary arteries. It takes about 10 minutes and costs $75-150 (usually not covered by insurance).

### What the Score Means
- **0:** No calcified plaque. Very low risk for the next 5-10 years
- **1-99:** Mild plaque. Moderate risk
- **100-399:** Moderate plaque. Elevated risk
- **400+:** Extensive plaque. High risk

A score of 0 is very reassuring — your 10-year risk of a heart event is less than 2%, even if other risk factors are present. A high score, even with "normal" cholesterol, means plaque is already there and treatment is urgent.

### Who Should Get a CAC Scan?
Best for people in the "gray zone" — intermediate risk where the decision to start medication is unclear. NOT recommended for people who are clearly low risk or clearly high risk.

## The Power of Prevention

### Lifestyle Changes Can Be Dramatic
The INTERHEART study found that 9 modifiable risk factors account for 90% of heart attack risk worldwide:
1. Abnormal lipids (high ApoB/ApoA1 ratio)
2. Smoking
3. Diabetes
4. Hypertension
5. Abdominal obesity
6. Psychosocial stress
7. Low fruit/vegetable intake
8. Low physical activity
9. Alcohol excess

Addressing ALL of these can reduce your risk by up to 90%.

### The Timeline of Change
When you make positive changes, benefits begin quickly:
- **Within 20 minutes of quitting smoking:** Heart rate drops
- **Within 1 year of quitting:** Heart disease risk is cut in half
- **After 2 weeks of exercise:** HDL begins to rise
- **After 3 months of diet change:** LDL can drop 10-15%
- **After 6 months of statin therapy:** Plaque growth can stabilize or reverse

### Aspirin: Not for Everyone
Low-dose aspirin (81mg) was once recommended for most adults. Current guidelines have changed:
- **NOT recommended** for primary prevention in most people (the bleeding risk outweighs benefits)
- **Still recommended** for people who have already had a heart attack or stroke (secondary prevention)
- **Talk to your doctor** before starting or stopping aspirin

### Blood Pressure: The Silent Partner
Hypertension works with cholesterol to accelerate heart disease:
- High blood pressure damages the inner lining of arteries
- This damage makes it easier for LDL to penetrate and form plaque
- Controlling blood pressure to below 130/80 significantly reduces risk
- Even small reductions (5-10 mmHg) have meaningful benefits

### Inflammation: The New Frontier
Chronic inflammation is increasingly recognized as a key driver of heart disease:
- **hs-CRP (high-sensitivity C-reactive protein):** A blood test that measures inflammation
  - Below 1 mg/L: Low risk
  - 1-3 mg/L: Average risk
  - Above 3 mg/L: High risk
- The CANTOS trial showed that reducing inflammation (with an anti-inflammatory drug) reduced heart attacks, even without changing cholesterol
- Anti-inflammatory lifestyle factors: Mediterranean diet, exercise, adequate sleep, stress management

## What to Discuss with Your Doctor

### Essential Questions
1. "What is my 10-year cardiovascular risk?"
2. "What are my ApoB and Lp(a) levels?" (if never tested)
3. "Should I get a coronary artery calcium scan?"
4. "Based on my risk, should I be on a statin?"
5. "What lifestyle changes would have the biggest impact for me?"
6. "How often should I get my lipids rechecked?"

### Red Flags to Report Immediately
- Chest pain or pressure (especially with exertion)
- Shortness of breath (new or worsening)
- Pain that radiates to jaw, arm, or back
- Sudden numbness or weakness on one side
- Sudden severe headache
- Unexplained dizziness or fainting

Heart attacks in women often present differently — fatigue, nausea, jaw pain, and shortness of breath may occur WITHOUT chest pain.
