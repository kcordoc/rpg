# Medications for Heart Health

## Statins: The First Line of Defense

Statins are the most widely prescribed cholesterol-lowering drugs in the world. Over 200 million people take them globally.

### How Statins Work
Statins block an enzyme called HMG-CoA reductase in the liver. This enzyme is a key step in cholesterol production. When the liver produces less cholesterol, it pulls more LDL from the bloodstream to compensate — lowering your LDL levels.

### Statin Effectiveness
- **High-intensity statins** (atorvastatin 40-80mg, rosuvastatin 20-40mg): Lower LDL by 50% or more
- **Moderate-intensity statins** (atorvastatin 10-20mg, simvastatin 20-40mg): Lower LDL by 30-49%
- **Low-intensity statins** (simvastatin 10mg, pravastatin 10-20mg): Lower LDL by less than 30%

### The Statin Controversy
Statins are one of the most studied drugs in history, with over 170,000 patients in randomized trials. The evidence shows:
- They reduce heart attacks by about 25-35%
- They reduce strokes by about 15-25%
- They reduce cardiovascular death by about 15-20%
- Benefits increase with longer use

### Common Side Effects
- **Muscle pain (myalgia):** Reported by 5-10% of patients. In controlled trials, about 1-2% have actual statin-related muscle problems (the rest is "nocebo effect" — expecting side effects causes them)
- **Liver enzyme elevation:** Rare and usually mild. Routine liver monitoring is no longer recommended for most patients
- **Slight increase in diabetes risk:** About 1 extra case per 255 patients treated for 4 years. The cardiovascular benefits far outweigh this risk
- **Memory complaints:** Not supported by randomized trial data. The FDA added a warning label, but large studies show no cognitive decline

### The Nocebo Effect
Studies where patients didn't know if they were taking a statin or placebo show that muscle pain rates are nearly identical in both groups. One study (SAMSON trial) found that 90% of statin side effects were attributable to the nocebo effect — people felt side effects because they expected them.

## Beyond Statins

### Ezetimibe (Zetia)
Works in the intestine to block cholesterol absorption. Lowers LDL by an additional 15-20% when added to a statin. Few side effects. Often the first drug added when a statin alone isn't enough.

### PCSK9 Inhibitors
These are injectable antibodies (evolocumab/Repatha, alirocumab/Praluent) that dramatically lower LDL:
- Lower LDL by 50-60% on top of statin therapy
- Can bring LDL below 25 mg/dL
- Given by injection every 2-4 weeks
- Very effective but expensive (though costs are dropping)
- Studies show additional reduction in heart attacks

How they work: PCSK9 is a protein that destroys LDL receptors on the liver. By blocking PCSK9, more receptors survive, and more LDL gets pulled from the blood.

### Bempedoic Acid (Nexletol)
A newer option for people who can't tolerate statins:
- Works in the liver (similar to statins) but doesn't affect muscles
- Lowers LDL by about 18% alone, 38% with ezetimibe
- FDA approved in 2020
- Particularly useful for statin-intolerant patients

### Inclisiran (Leqvio)
A revolutionary approach — small interfering RNA (siRNA) that silences the PCSK9 gene:
- Given by injection just twice a year (after initial doses)
- Lowers LDL by about 50%
- Administered in the doctor's office
- FDA approved in 2021

### Fibrates
Primarily lower triglycerides (by 20-50%) and modestly raise HDL. Examples: fenofibrate, gemfibrozil. Most useful for people with very high triglycerides (above 500 mg/dL) to prevent pancreatitis.

### Omega-3 Fatty Acids (Prescription Strength)
Icosapent ethyl (Vascepa) at high doses (4g/day) reduced cardiovascular events by 25% in the REDUCE-IT trial. Regular fish oil supplements at lower doses have NOT shown the same benefit.

## Who Needs Medication?

The decision to start cholesterol medication isn't just about your cholesterol number. Doctors use risk calculators that consider:
- Age
- Sex
- Race
- Total cholesterol and HDL
- Blood pressure
- Diabetes status
- Smoking status

If your 10-year risk of a cardiovascular event is above 7.5%, guidelines recommend considering statin therapy. If it's above 20%, statin therapy is strongly recommended.

## How Low Should LDL Go?

There's growing evidence that "lower is better" with no floor:
- People with genetic mutations causing very low LDL (15-30 mg/dL) from birth are extremely healthy
- No adverse effects have been seen at very low LDL levels in clinical trials
- The 2023 European guidelines recommend LDL below 55 mg/dL for high-risk patients

The concept of "too low" cholesterol is a myth — your body can make all the cholesterol your cells need even when blood levels are very low.
