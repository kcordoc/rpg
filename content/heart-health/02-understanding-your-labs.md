# Understanding Your Lab Results

A lipid panel is a blood test that measures the fats and fatty substances in your blood. It's the most common test for assessing heart disease risk. Here's what each number means and why it matters.

## The Standard Lipid Panel

When you get blood work done, your lipid panel typically includes four measurements:

### Total Cholesterol
This is the sum of all cholesterol in your blood. It includes LDL, HDL, and 20% of your triglycerides.

- **Desirable:** Less than 200 mg/dL
- **Borderline high:** 200-239 mg/dL
- **High:** 240 mg/dL and above

But total cholesterol alone is misleading. You could have high total cholesterol because of high HDL (which is good!) or because of high LDL (which is bad). That's why the breakdown matters more than the total.

### LDL Cholesterol (the "bad" one)
- **Optimal:** Less than 100 mg/dL
- **Near optimal:** 100-129 mg/dL
- **Borderline high:** 130-159 mg/dL
- **High:** 160-189 mg/dL
- **Very high:** 190 mg/dL and above

For people with heart disease or diabetes, doctors often aim for LDL below 70 mg/dL.

### HDL Cholesterol (the "good" one)
- **Low (higher risk):** Less than 40 mg/dL (men) or 50 mg/dL (women)
- **Normal:** 40-59 mg/dL
- **High (protective):** 60 mg/dL and above

Every 1 mg/dL increase in HDL is associated with a 2-3% decrease in heart disease risk.

### Triglycerides
- **Normal:** Less than 150 mg/dL
- **Borderline high:** 150-199 mg/dL
- **High:** 200-499 mg/dL
- **Very high:** 500 mg/dL and above

High triglycerides are strongly linked to metabolic syndrome, diabetes, and pancreatitis.

## Fasting vs. Non-Fasting Tests

Traditionally, lipid panels required fasting for 9-12 hours. However, recent research shows that non-fasting lipid panels are nearly as accurate for most measurements. The main exception is triglycerides, which can spike after meals.

Many doctors now accept non-fasting tests for routine screening. But if your triglycerides come back high on a non-fasting test, they'll usually ask you to repeat it fasting.

## Advanced Lipid Testing

Standard lipid panels miss some important details. Advanced tests can provide a more complete picture:

### ApoB (Apolipoprotein B)
ApoB is a protein found on LDL particles. Each LDL particle has exactly one ApoB molecule, so ApoB count = LDL particle count. This is important because:
- Two people can have the same LDL cholesterol level but very different numbers of LDL particles
- The person with MORE particles (higher ApoB) has higher risk
- Optimal ApoB is below 90 mg/dL; ideal is below 80 mg/dL

Think of it this way: LDL cholesterol measures the "cargo" (cholesterol), while ApoB counts the "trucks" (particles). More trucks means more chances of cholesterol getting stuck in artery walls.

### Lp(a) — Lipoprotein(a)
Lp(a) is a special type of LDL particle that's particularly dangerous. It's:
- Genetically determined — your levels are largely set by your DNA
- Not affected by diet or exercise
- A strong independent risk factor for heart disease
- Present in about 20% of the population at elevated levels

You should get tested at least once in your lifetime. High Lp(a) is above 50 mg/dL (or above 125 nmol/L). Currently, there are no FDA-approved medications specifically for Lp(a), but several are in clinical trials.

### Non-HDL Cholesterol
This is simply: Total Cholesterol minus HDL. It captures ALL the "bad" cholesterol-carrying particles (LDL + VLDL + IDL + Lp(a)). Many cardiologists consider non-HDL cholesterol a better predictor than LDL alone.

- **Optimal:** Less than 130 mg/dL
- **For high-risk patients:** Less than 100 mg/dL

## How Often Should You Get Tested?

- **Adults 20+:** Every 4-6 years if low risk
- **Men 45+ and Women 55+:** Every 1-2 years
- **People with risk factors:** Annually or as directed by your doctor
- **People on cholesterol medications:** Every 3-12 months to monitor treatment

## What Affects Your Results?

Several things can temporarily affect your lipid panel:
- **Recent illness or surgery** can lower LDL temporarily
- **Pregnancy** raises cholesterol (test 6 weeks postpartum)
- **Certain medications** (steroids, birth control pills, diuretics)
- **Rapid weight loss** can temporarily raise cholesterol
- **Time of year** — cholesterol tends to be higher in winter
