# Diet, Exercise, and Lifestyle

## How Diet Affects Cholesterol

The relationship between diet and cholesterol is more nuanced than most people think. Here's what the science actually says:

### Saturated Fat: The Primary Dietary Villain
Saturated fat — found in red meat, butter, cheese, and coconut oil — raises LDL cholesterol more than any other dietary factor. Replacing saturated fat with unsaturated fat can lower LDL by 10-15%.

The mechanism: saturated fat reduces the number of LDL receptors on liver cells, so less LDL gets cleared from the blood.

### Dietary Cholesterol: Less Important Than You Think
The 2020 Dietary Guidelines removed the previous limit of 300mg of dietary cholesterol per day. For most people, eating cholesterol has a surprisingly small effect on blood cholesterol because the liver adjusts its production.

However, about 25% of people are "hyper-responders" — their blood cholesterol rises significantly with dietary cholesterol. There's no easy way to know which group you're in without testing.

### Trans Fats: The Real Danger
Artificial trans fats (partially hydrogenated oils) are the worst dietary fat for your heart. They simultaneously raise LDL and LOWER HDL. The FDA banned them in 2018, but some processed foods may still contain small amounts.

### The Mediterranean Diet
The Mediterranean diet has the strongest evidence for heart disease prevention:
- Rich in olive oil, nuts, fish, fruits, vegetables, and whole grains
- Limited red meat and processed foods
- Moderate wine consumption (optional)

The PREDIMED trial showed a Mediterranean diet supplemented with extra-virgin olive oil or nuts reduced heart attacks, strokes, and cardiovascular death by about 30% compared to a low-fat diet.

### Specific Foods That Lower Cholesterol
- **Oats and barley:** Contain beta-glucan fiber that binds cholesterol in the gut
- **Nuts:** Walnuts and almonds can lower LDL by 5-10%
- **Fatty fish:** Omega-3 fatty acids lower triglycerides by 15-30%
- **Plant sterols/stanols:** Found in fortified foods, can lower LDL by 6-15%
- **Soy protein:** Can modestly lower LDL by 3-5%
- **Psyllium fiber:** 10g/day can lower LDL by 7%

## Exercise and Cholesterol

### How Exercise Helps
Regular exercise primarily raises HDL and lowers triglycerides. Its effect on LDL is modest but real.

- **HDL increase:** 5-10% with regular aerobic exercise
- **Triglyceride decrease:** 15-30% with regular exercise
- **LDL decrease:** 5-10% with vigorous exercise

### How Much Exercise?
The American Heart Association recommends:
- **150 minutes per week** of moderate aerobic activity (brisk walking, cycling), OR
- **75 minutes per week** of vigorous aerobic activity (running, swimming laps)
- **Plus** muscle-strengthening activities 2+ days per week

Even modest amounts of exercise help. Walking 30 minutes a day, 5 days a week, can significantly improve your lipid profile.

### The Runner's Paradox
Interestingly, extreme endurance exercise (ultramarathons, Ironman) doesn't necessarily lead to better cholesterol numbers. Some elite athletes have elevated LDL. Exercise improves the FUNCTION of your cardiovascular system beyond just cholesterol numbers.

## Other Lifestyle Factors

### Smoking
Smoking lowers HDL by an average of 5-9 mg/dL. It also damages the lining of arteries, making it easier for LDL to penetrate and form plaque. Quitting smoking raises HDL within weeks and reduces heart disease risk by 50% within one year.

### Alcohol
Moderate alcohol consumption (1 drink/day for women, 2 for men) is associated with higher HDL. However, alcohol raises triglycerides. More than moderate drinking increases heart disease risk. The latest research suggests even moderate drinking may have fewer benefits than previously thought.

### Sleep
Poor sleep quality and sleep apnea are linked to worse lipid profiles. People who sleep less than 6 hours per night tend to have higher triglycerides and lower HDL. Treating sleep apnea with CPAP can improve cholesterol numbers.

### Stress
Chronic stress raises cortisol, which increases triglycerides and LDL. The mechanism involves stress-induced eating, reduced exercise, and direct hormonal effects on fat metabolism. Stress management techniques (meditation, yoga) can modestly improve lipid profiles.

### Weight Loss
Losing weight, especially visceral fat (belly fat), significantly improves lipid profiles:
- Every 10 pounds lost → LDL drops by about 5-8 mg/dL
- Weight loss raises HDL and dramatically lowers triglycerides
- Even a 5-10% body weight reduction shows meaningful improvement
